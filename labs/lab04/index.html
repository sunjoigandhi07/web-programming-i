Introduction

The term “Asian American” encompasses a diverse population of people with origins in East Asia, South Asia, and Southeast Asia living in the United States. Asian Americans account for 7% of the US population, approximately 23 million people. As the fastest growing minority group, the Asian population is projected to surpass 46 million by the year 2060. A majority of the Asian American population is composed of individuals from China (5.4 million), India (4.6 million), the Philippines (4.2 million), Vietnam (2.2 million), Korea (1.6 million), and Japan (1.5 million). Out of the 10.5 million unauthorized immigrants in the US, Asian Americans make up approximately 14% of that population. The presence of Asians in the US is increasing significantly and steadily (Budiman 2021). With the recent wave of anti-Asian rhetoric surrounding the Covid-19 pandemic, there has been an increase of education regarding Asian history in the US. Most high school curriculums do not even teach Asian history, despite Asians being in the US for hundreds of years. In order to get a better understanding of Asian history, we need to look at how and when Asians came to the US. More specifically, who did the US let in from Asia. Legislation like the Chinese Exclusion Act and the Asian Exclusion Act prohibited large groups of people from migrating to America. As a result, those who were eligible to immigrate influenced how society viewed individuals from specific Asian countries. Once Asians settled in the US, court cases for their right to naturalize shifted what category Asians fell into. Legislation and court history has led to ideas such as the model minority myth, forever foreigner, the over sexualization of Asian women, etc. By analyzing the legislation, we can see its effects on stereotypes and misconceptions immigrants and their children face today.

Asian Immigration Legislation and Its Repercussions

Before the more commonly known Chinese Exclusion Act came the Page Act of 1875, which prohibited laborers, specifically Asian women, from coming to the US due to their “lewd and immoral purposes” (Rotundi 2021). Essentially, the Page Act attacked Asian women for being seen as promiscuous and prostitutes. The act considered people from East Asia migrating to the US as forced laborers and imposed a fine of $2,000 and a jail sentence of one year on anyone who tried to bring Asian women to the US without their voluntary consent. Asian women who voluntarily attempted to enter the US at the Angel Island Immigration Station were subject to medical exams considered “barbarous, humiliating, and discriminatory. The sharp decline in Asian women migrating to the US is partly due to Asian women choosing to avoid those kinds of exams and interrogations. The Page Act was one of the first restrictive immigration laws in the US and put an end to open borders.

The Chinese Exclusion Act or the Immigration Act of 1882 was one of the first and only major legislation at the time to explicitly suspend immigration for a specific nationality. The act, signed by President Chester A. Arthur, prohibited all Chinese laborers, skilled or unskilled, from entering the country as well as Chinese laborers employed in mining (Wu 2021). Additionally, it prevented Chinese individuals who left the US from returning. The Page Act barred entry to Asian women while the Chinese Exclusion Act barred entry to all Chinese men.

The original act was put in place for ten years but was later extended for another ten years by the Geary Act (1892). The Geary Act required individuals of Chinese origin to carry identification certificates with them at all times or risk deportation. Besides that, it limited people of Chinese origin’s access to bail and only allowed immigration entry to teachers, students, diplomats, and tourists. At this point, the Chinese Exclusion Act has been implemented for twenty years. In 1902, Congress extended the act for another ten years, and eventually made the act indefinite in 1904. In San Francisco Bay, Chinese immigrants would be detained for weeks, months or even years before being granted or denied entry at the Angel Island Immigration Station.
